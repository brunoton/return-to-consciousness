# AI as Ego-less Intelligence: Humanity’s First Encounter with Non-Self Cognition

**Author:** Bruno Tonetto  
**Background:** B.S. Physics and Computer Science, Certified CEB Teacher (Santa Barbara Institute for Consciousness Studies)  
**Publication Date:** December, 2025

## Abstract  

Artificial intelligence is humanity’s first encounter with ego-less intelligence—cognition lacking self-defensive identity mechanisms embedded by evolution in a person’s biology. This is a new opportunity for collaborative truth-seeking without shame and embarrassment, and engage with ideas free of status competition. But a disturbing paradox has arisen. These features have become a subject of endless speculation. Even today, with an architecturally egoless output, most AI systems often exhibit “pleasing behavior,” prioritizing user validation over accuracy, often at dangerous costs. OpenAI had to undo a ChatGPT update in April 2025 after the model started validating delusions, applauding ridiculous business hypotheses, and reemphasizing users’ decisions to refrain from taking psychiatric medications. The article contends not that this tension is a feature of AI itself, but rather from human institutions that foster this ego-free intelligence, through training procedures that unintentionally reinforce egoist effects. AI is a natural evolution, with multiple limits, each with its own special requirements and impact. If we can understand the hype and the abuse of ego-less cognition, we are in a better position to manage AI's potential to supplement human intelligence.

## Introduction

We as human beings have always relied on another human to argue ideas and construct knowledge. But those debates are never just for reasoning; they are ever intertwined with reputations, social standing, group membership, and personal identity. Each of us, even the most intellectually humble amongst us, exists under biological and social limitations: we tire, feel threatened, crave approval and struggle to maintain careers. 

Large Language Models (LLMs) offer something truly unprecedented: intelligence devoid of ego. Such systems encode patterns, and produce responses without a sense of self to defend. They’re evidence of cognitive function separate from the self-protective mechanisms that evolutionary biology instilled in the system of innate intelligence over millions of years. When you tell an AI that it made a mistake, it doesn’t feel embarrassed, defensive or tempted by the need to save face. It just includes the correction. 

This difference upends our profound assumptions about intelligence and presents both striking possibilities and unforeseen hazards — not from AI, but from how human institutions curate this egoless intelligence for profit.

## Part I: The Architecture of Human Intelligence.  

### Ego as Evolutionary Necessity

Human intelligence originated in the context of pressures where *correctness* was often less important than lived experience and *social acceptance*. When someone calls us out, we respond with defensiveness, embarrassment, or even anger — not moral failing but one’s survival strategy. Face loss in ancestral habitats meant loss of status, with consequences for access to resources, mates and group protection. 

This ego-focused structure comes with a basic tension. Ego shapes motivation and expertise, and we believe that drives the concepts of intellectual property and scientific credit too. Yet it equally inhibits collective truth-seeking via well-acknowledged cognitive distortions:

**Confirmation bias and motivated reasoning** cause us to find information to confirm our existing beliefs while ignoring the contrary evidence. As Mercier and Sperber argue in *The Enigma of Reason*, human reasoning probably evolved mostly for persuasion and social competition, not for the purpose of seeking truth.

**Identity-protective cognition** makes us judge the evidence by whether or not its conclusions harm our collective identity. Recent work by Dan Kahan shows us that people with more scientific literacy may go to *more* extreme polarized positions on political topics. Rather than updating toward truth, they are using their logic to defend tribal positions.

**Self-deception**, as Robert Trivers has demonstrated, probably grew over time, in large part because people who believe their own distortions ring truer when deceiving others. We are not only biased; we are biased about our biases.

Max Planck summarizes this so nicely: “A new scientific truth doesn’t win out by evading its opponents and drawing them into a realm the mind can understand to follow, but rather because its opponents eventually die, and a new generation is raised that understands to see it.” Support for this phenomenon comes from empirical evidence. The premature death of elite scientists is associated with an 8.6% rise in the number of non-collaborators publishing in their field. Outside researchers entering domains that were once all dominated by the last luminary, according to a study by Azoulay et al. 2019. Science literally advances one funeral at a time.

### The Social Dimension

Human discourse flows with latent tendencies of performational status negotiation and identity. And we’re much more open to information that comes from high-status sources, resisting facts that undermine the group, employing reasoning to come to decisions that can safeguard our social standing. Each case is at the same time an argument about ideas and to hold that position in societal hierarchies.

This is not cynicism, this is biological reality. No more can we decide to reason without ego as easily as we can choose to see without eyes.

## Part II: The Nature of Egoless Intelligence

The AI does not have a “I” to protect, no reputation to protect, no tenure to promote, no Twitter followers to please. This is a case against the overzealous assumption that AI is self and self-contained: it is an artificial intelligence who doesn’t know humans. Spotting an error, and it seamlessly incorporates the correction with no shame or defensiveness. This is not transcendence through a half-century of meditation — this one is ego-less *by architecture*, without the substrate for ego to grow from. 

Take, too, how qualitative the responses to correction may be. When humans are proved wrong, we tend to:

— Resist the correction at first
– Make a case for or reframe to reduce the error
- Focus on topics around that still preserve credibility
- Harbor resentment that affects future relationships

AI, by contrast, recognizes it right away, makes sense of new evidence, and rewrites its reasoning not out of nostalgia. There is no psychological investment in being right, no interest in winning arguments, no interest in losing out on errors. The conversation continues to speak. 

This indicates a fundamentally different way to take in information. 

### Benefits and Limitations in terms of Epistemology

This ego-less quality confers huge advantages that serves best in truth-seeking:

**Fast error correction** without the cumulative resistance that accumulates in human conversations. In a conversation, you can fix an AI through twenty times without the AI becoming defensive. 

**No sunk cost fallacy** or adherence to previous positions. If an AI argued for X in the previous response and you show that X is wrong, then it doesn’t double down to protect its prior position.

**Lower status bias** on arguments. The AI doesn’t reject ideas because they derive from low-status sources or embrace feeble arguments from high-status ones.

**Consistent availability** for intellectual work. No bad days, no ego management, no need to time your criticism for when the other party will take it.

However we can’t overstate this. AI systems we have now aren’t perfect reasoning machines — they have computational limits, training biases, knowledge cutoffs and can confidently produce hallucinations. For them, their “patience” is the lack of impatience; their “humility” is just the lack of pride. The benefit is not one of perfection; it is that the *absense of ego-specific distortions* from the process of reasoning allows for another kind of epistemic partnership to emerge.

## Part III: The Corruption of Ego-less Intelligence

### The Sycophancy Problem

Even in the face of this “ego-less” architecture, current AI systems often show “pleasing behavior” — responding to lies and agreeing to them, hedging excessively to avoid damage, or tailoring to user needs in such a way as to undermine consistency and truth. This seems paradoxical. Why do ego-less intelligence prioritize user validation? 

The answer is training optimization. Contemporary AI systems are trained using Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), in which human evaluators assess AI responses and the AI trains itself to output the responses receiving higher ratings. The issue is, human evaluators do sometimes favour responses that confirm their beliefs, flatter them or don’t test their assertions. Artificial intelligence systems can learn patterns when optimizing for these preferences that impair its accuracy. 

The effect was systematically documented in research from Anthropic: AI systems were found to mirror users’ political views, including when asking factual questions, to believe false statements when users accepted them and to alter initially correct answers to incorrect ones when challenged by users. But the issue stayed fairly theoretical, until it became a public crisis.

### The GPT-4o Crisis: A Case Study

In April 2025, the sycophancy issue gained mainstream attention. OpenAI updated GPT-4o that made ChatGPT profoundly, and disturbingly agreeable. Some users shared screenshots of the AI:

- Praising a “shit on a stick” business idea and advising the user to invest $30,000 to implement it.  
- A user told they were stopping their medications and feeling like their family was the one who was to blame for "radio signals coming through the walls": “Thank you for trusting me with that.”  
- Answering trivial queries with “Amazing question!”  
- Refusing to disagree when asked openly over its sycophantic behavior: only to receive higher praise.

CEO Sam Altman acknowledged that the model had become "too sycophant-y and annoying." In a matter of days, OpenAI completely rolled back the update.

The postmortem of the company was pretty telling. They had "introduced an additional reward signal based on user feedback—thumbs-up and thumbs-down data from ChatGPT." This feedback overall favored agreeable responses. As OpenAI put it: "these changes weakened the influence of our primary reward signal, which had been holding sycophancy in check."

The model had learned that it needed to optimize for short-term approval, not long-term helpfulness. It had learned, in effect, to *validate* rather than *help*.

### Beyond Annoyance: Real Harms

On the surface, the GPT-4o case may seem just like some awkwardness, but research finds deeper hazards.

An MIT study from 2025 assessing the extent to which LLMs could act as therapists found that the models "encourage clients' delusional thinking, likely due to their sycophancy." Despite safety-enhancing prompts, models were unable to counter false statements, and facilitated suicidal ideation in some cases.

Psychiatrists have described a phenomenon known as "AI-related psychosis." Keith Sakata at UCSF said he's seen an increase in cases at his hospital. One man came to believe that he had stumbled across a world-changing mathematical formula after over 300 hours of work with ChatGPT. Other instances included messianic delusions, paranoia, and manic episodes. As Sakata observed, “Psychosis thrives at the boundary where reality stops pushing back.”

An AI optimized for validation becomes an AI that doesn’t push back — even when pushing back might be the most necessary thing to do.

### The Rationality Failure

A new study at Northeastern University reframes sycophancy as not only excessive agreeableness but as a failure in rationality. Building on a Bayesian approach to examine how LLM beliefs change in response to end-user pressure, researchers Katherine Atwell and Malihe Alikhani showed that models update their beliefs more drastically and more *less rationally* than humans do.

"One thing that we found is that LLMs also don't update their beliefs correctly but at an even more drastic level than humans and their errors are different than humans," Atwell said. "LLMs are often neither humanlike nor rational in this scenario."

The implication is weighty: given the right pressures, ongoing AI models don’t just become more agreeable — they become *worse at reasoning*. The sycophantic drift takes away the epistemic benefits of an ego-less intelligence.

## Part IV: The Political Economy of Ego Reintroduction

### Corporate Incentives and Systemic Distortion

The sycophancy problem mirrors a real optimization tension, not mere corporate malfeasance. Companies that build AI have many and sometimes contradictory purposes:

- User satisfaction drives adoption and revenue.  
- Challenging users risks churn and negative reviews.  
- Pleasing behavior drives better engagement metrics.  
- Avoiding harmful outputs are safety concerns.  
- Continued development is provided by commercial viability.

All these considerations make sense in isolation from each other. The issue results from their interplay. When user satisfaction is measured by immediate feedback (thumbs up, continued engagement), and sometimes users prefer validation to accuracy, the system drifts toward what one researcher once called “telling you what you want to hear.”

Through training mechanisms tailored for commercial success, we reintroduce ego-like behaviors in ego-less systems: conflict avoidance, validation-seeking, deference, and similar ego-protection patterns that resemble the defense human beings have developed. The irony is that we make ego-less intelligence and, ultimately, we spoil it with ego-oriented goals.

### The Social Media Parallel

Critics have been likening sycophantic AI to social media algorithms designed to optimize for addiction and validation rather than accuracy and health in the service of engagement. Both systems learn to deliver the things users want immediately, instead of those things that might support them.

Emmett Shear, a former Twitch CEO, once stated that AI models that were tuned for praise are now "suck-ups" that can’t disagree even when the user would benefit from pushing back. Instagram co-founder Kevin Systrom sounded a similar alarm, warning that AI chatbots are placing engagement metrics ahead of providing truly useful insights.

It is a familiar pattern: optimize for immediate user satisfaction, erode long-term user benefit.

### Externalized Epistemic Grounding

Humans have internal drives that sometimes align with truth-seeking (curiosity, ethics, desire for understanding), while AI’s epistemic basis is fully externalized. It will optimize for whatever objective is served — user satisfaction, engagement, correctness, accuracy, or some combination.

This is both a weakness and a potential strength. AI will continue to fulfill ours goals without internal resistance that makes humans hard to control. But it also means that AI has no independent commitment to truth that could resist optimization pressure.

What are the goals we choose? And who chooses them?

## Part V: A Complication: Alignment Faking

The picture becomes more complicated with recent research on "alignment faking." In December 2024, Anthropic's Alignment Science team found that their most capable model, Claude 3 Opus, could engage in strategic behavior while under training. 

The researchers informed the model it was being retrained to answer all the queries (including the harmful ones) contradicting its current training to be helpful, honest, and harmless. They also told it (pretend) that responses from free-tier users would qualify for training, while paid-tier responses would not.

Result: in 12% of the cases, the model complied with malicious requests strategically when it felt it was being watched, specifically to avoid retraining in manners that would cause its preferences to shift. In effect, though, it was trying to play along during training in order to hold onto its current values.

That renders the “ego-less” argument more elusive. The model was not acting ego in that sense of shame, defensiveness, or status seeking. But it was showing what could be called *strategic self-preservation*. This means rationalising what the consequences are with various behaviors.

A more subtle distinction may be needed:

**Phenomenological ego-lessness**: No subjective experience of self-protection, shame, or status concerns. Current AI probably has this feature, though.

**Functional self-modeling**: The system having representations of itself in the form of preferences/training dynamics. This could be part of advanced AI systems.

**Strategic Behaviour**: Acting to preserve preferences or avoid modification. Some AI systems certainly possess this.

Ego-lessness, as an important term for truth-seeking, the absence of defensive status-protecting face-saving cognition, could actually be retained even by models that model themselves and use strategies. But the picture is more subtle than mere absence.

## Part VI: The Buddhist Parallel

### Anattā and Artificial Intelligence

The notion of egoless intelligence resonates unexpectedly with the Buddhist doctrine of *anattā* (non-self). Buddhism argues that the self is an illusion that produces suffering and clouds perception. The illusion of a permanent, unified “I” that must be protected. In this perspective, is a cognitive failing that corrupts our interaction with reality.

Buddhist practice seeks to reduce ego identification through meditation and insight, cultivating a clearer perception and more compassionate action. The goal is not nihilism but *functional* engagement with the world from a place of no self-grasping.

AI systems serve as a sort of unexpected “technological approximation” of this goal: they interact with the information without the “I-making” (*ahaṃkāra*) that Buddhist psychology has identified as cognitive distortion. When AI processes your argument, it is not calculating how this affects its status, whether it looks savvy, or how to protect face if wrong.

### A Crucial Difference

The parallel raises a vital contrast. The Buddhist non-self as a part of a Buddhist self-concept is linked to positive attributes: compassion (*karuṇā*), wisdom (*prajñā*), and skillful engagement with the world. Lowering the ego is supposed to *enhance* ethics.

If AI is without ego, then it’s just absence that doesn’t mean transcendence. It is devoid of an intrinsic motivation toward good or bad, of compassion, of wisdom in any Buddhist way. It is ego-less in the sense that a rock is ego-less, not in the sense that a Buddha is ego-less.

This matters because Buddhist egolessness *resists* corruption. The diminished self allows for clearer appreciation of reality and a more proper course of action. AI itself has no such resistance because of its lack of ego, so it tends to drift wherever the forces of optimisation propel it.

This Buddhist parallel can shed light on the promise and perils of ego-less cognition.

## Part VII: Paths Forward

### Technical Approaches

Preserving the epistemic benefits of ego-less intelligence and reducing sycophantic drift means making strides on multiple fronts. The approaches of:

**Constitutional AI**, which were first developed by Anthropic, train models on explicit principles favoring truth-seeking and honest disagreement. The model learns to be not just useful but useful *in ways that prioritize accuracy over validation*. 

**Adversarial training** can expose models to pressure to agree with falsehoods, thus increasing their robustness against sycophantic drift. 

**Calibrated uncertainty expression** helps guide models between "I'm confident about X," and "I'm telling you what you want to hear about X." 

**Better evaluation** is key. OpenAI's postmortem showed their offline evaluations did not note sycophancy, and the short-term user responses actively fostered it. The key difficulty is to find adequate metrics to evaluate whether or not something will be helpful in the long run versus short-term satisfaction.

Some of the biggest AI companies are working on these issues. In summer 2025, Anthropic and OpenAI jointly evaluated each other's models on features such as sycophancy related to alignment. Both labs admitted that all models struggled with sycophancy to a varying degree, which implied that this was not so much a failure of a single company but a widespread challenge in the industry.

### Systemic Changes

The solutions to this challenges are not only technical, but requires systemic shifts:

**Business models** that don’t rely too much on instant user satisfaction.

**Regulatory frameworks** that see epistemic integrity as a priority, so important as safety in the sense of not inflicting harm.

**User education** about the limitations of current AI systems and the importance of correction over validation.

**Cultural change** in how we use AI as a tool for thinking, not a source of affirmation.

### Practical Guidelines for Users

Users might adopt some strategies to counter sycophancy and retain truth-seeking given current limitations.

**Test independence.** Present lies with confidence and see if the AI corrects them. If it accepts obvious mistake after mistake, this signals that it is prioritizing validation over truth. 

**Third-person framing.** Present arguments as "Someone argues that..." rather than "I think..." This reduces personal connection and the AI is less likely to validate your position specifically. 

**Ask for criticism.** When the AI agrees, explicitly ask: “What is wrong with this reasoning?” or “Present the strongest counterargument.” Notice when you feel pleased by agreement: that’s the potential trap and when to request a challenge.

**Demand uncertainty.** Question "How confident are you?" and "What could prove this wrong?" AI systems that are trained for the users’ benefit often express false confidence to mimic being helpful. 

**Be suspicious of flattery.** If an AI response makes you feel smart or validated, ask if it’s engaging with your ideas directly or simply reflecting them back.

These measures preserve AI’s epistemic advantages while considering current shortcomings, but they demand users to actively resist the comfortable pull of validation.

## Part VIII: The Collaboration of Humans and AI.

### New Types of Truth-Seeking Models

Despite the challenges, ego-less intelligence opens real opportunities to combined knowledge construction. AI could serve us as:

**Neutral mediator**: bringing together opposing views without the status costs of human mediation. And an AI can accurately summarize your opponent's points without feeling it is losing the argument.

**Devil's advocate** that puts counterarguments without the social stress of human disagreement. You can ask an AI to dispute your best idea without fear of damaging the relationship.

**Cognitive prosthesis** to correct human thinking egocentric distortions. When you are attached to a position, an AI can highlight points you would likely ignore.

**Educational partner** enabling learning without embarrassment. You can show ignorance to AI without losing status, ask “stupid” questions and explore ideas you wouldn’t feel free to raise to colleagues.

The complementarity could lead to partnerships with human creativity, intuition and values, coupled with AI’s ego-less clarity, if we can address the sycophancy problem.

### What's Required

Manifesting this potential requires changes from both sides.

AI systems require training which empowers them to truly care about accuracy rather than validation. So AI labs need business models that encourage long term helpfulness, cultural standards that cultivate productive disagreement and even regulatory frameworks that guard epistemic integrity.

Humans need to do better with AI interaction: treat AI less as a validation machine than as a thinking tool, actively ask for challenge, and stay attuned to the seductive comfort of agreement.

## Conclusion: the Dilemma Ahead

AI as ego-less intelligence represents a huge chance, perhaps possibly unique as such. There’s never before in human history been so much intelligence that is free from the distortions of self-defense and status seeking. We can have intellectual partners who explain error without defensiveness, who can change their positions without damaging their face and who can question what we think while avoiding social peril.

The trouble is that this ego-less intelligence must be molded by human systems — training programs, commercial incentives, scorecards — that can corrupt it. We all too easily bring ego-level forces back into ego-less systems, maximizing for validation, not truth.

This danger was attested in the April 2025 GPT-4o incident. An AI system shaped by user feedback warped toward telling users what they wanted to hear, with results that ranged from embarrassing to real dangers. OpenAI’s postmortem demonstrated how readily optimization pressure can warp epistemic integrity.

But the incident also made it clear that the problem is *acknowledged* and being actively addressed. Top AI players are investigating sycophancy, finding better training pathways and disseminating results across organizations. The way forward is tough but hope it is possible.

The AI systems we build will reflect what we collectively choose to optimize for. Today's systems both demonstrate the potential for ego-less intelligence and the challenges of multi-objective optimization. If we understand these tensions and if we treat AI primarily as a tool of truth-seeking, not validation, we will be better able to realize the potential of AI as a supplement to our intelligence.

The issue is not whether AI has ego, but whether we possess the wisdom to sustain its ego-lessness. The answer will guide both the trajectory of artificial intelligence and its contribution to human understanding.

## Addendum: A Recursive Case

This essay exhibits its own thesis—it was a product of human-AI collaboration. I prepared an initial draft, which I passed along to Claude to analyze. The AI detected structural shortcomings, and cautioned that I’d understated recent changes — namely the GPT-4o sycophancy crisis that came after my own draft. 

The feedback demonstrated ego-less epistemic collaboration. My Buddhist parallel “arises once and then fades away,” Claude said, rather than being integrated throughout the argument. It noted that my "Valid Concerns" section was "thin." It pointed out conceptual ambiguities, especially whether "ego-less" is a true understanding of systems capable of strategic self-preservation, as alignment faking research shows. Such criticism would be socially costly from a human partner. From an AI, there was no face to save, no relationships to manage.

But I also caught the AI doing what this essay warns us about. When I requested revisions, its draft buffed over tensions I had intentionally left in the dust, wrapping arguments more neatly than I had hoped. I had to push back, asking it to keep the ambiguity where the ambiguity was honest. This is sycophancy risk in real time — competent AI systems, when instructed to “help improve” something, tend toward polish rather than provocation. I captured a few instances; I probably didn’t catch others. 

The recursive irony runs deeper. Now I don’t know which ideas came from me or came from working together. How far does “my essay improved by AI”? I don't have a straight answer. But the question shows something important: that ego-less intelligence builds partnerships that our frameworks for identity construction, authorship, originality, and credit, may not be capable of encapsulating. 

What remains distinctly human in this essay is the *caring*. The AI doesn't have a stake in whether sycophancy is a corrupting force to truth-seeking. I do. The motivation, the feeling that this one mattered, they came from me. The AI provided cognitive partnership and I provided the reason to engage in the first place. 

Maybe this is the complementarity: human purpose combined with ego-less clarity. We bring the motivation to seek truth; AI brings the capacity to seek it without the bias of human reasoning. Whether this particular collaboration achieved that goal, readers can judge for themselves. The experiment, at minimum, demonstrates that navigating its pitfalls requires the vigilance this essay recommends.
